# Local Demand & Willingness-to-Pay — Portland Permit Radar (research 2026-07-17)

**Verdict: CAUTION, bordering on NO-GO** for the original framing — "a permit-data demand radar sold to *small residential contractors* as a prospecting tool." The data is easy to get; the problem is that small contractors mostly don't use it, can't work it, and won't pay much for it. There are narrow viable pockets (below), but they aren't the "help small local landscapers/remodelers" vision.

---

## The three compounding problems

### 1. The "already hired" timing problem (structural, not fixable)
In residential work, the **licensed contractor pulls the permit as "contractor of record" — which happens *after* the homeowner already hired them.** So by the time a permit is public, that homeowner isn't a prospect; they're someone else's customer. A permit radar aimed at *winning homeowner jobs* fights the data's own timing. (Sources: [Angi – who pulls permits](https://www.angi.com/articles/who-pays-building-permits.htm), [Jaspector](https://www.jaspector.com/articles/who-pulls-the-permit/).)

### 2. Small contractors don't use it, can't work it, and prefer inbound
- The real tools (Shovels, BuildZoom, Construction Monitor) are built for and bought by **suppliers, lenders, subs, and enterprise data teams — not small residential crews.**
- **Prospecting data converts poorly:** direct mail ~1–4.4% response; cold outreach 0.2–2% to close. Contractor forums are harsher ("5,000 mailers for ~0.2%", "never buy a list… biggest waste of time").
- **Even the permit vendors admit subscriptions go unused** — "80% of deals need 5+ follow-ups, 44% of salespeople give up after one." A one-truck operator has no sales muscle to work a cold list.
- **Contractors pay MORE for high-intent *inbound* "hire-me" leads** (Google LSA, referrals close at 40–60% vs 15–20% for shared/cold), paying **$53–$400 per lead** specifically because the customer already raised their hand.

### 3. The space is commoditized and undercut by free
- Free **PortlandMaps** exports permits as CSV/JSON; Oregon ePermitting (Accela) is public. A paid Portland-only tool competes with *free*.
- A cheap crowded tier already targets small contractors: **PermitStack $19/mo, Permit Ledger $39/mo, PermitGrab $149/mo, PermitMap.** Differentiation is thin because the underlying data is public.

## Where permit / prospecting data DOES work (the narrow pockets)
Every success case shares three traits: a **dedicated sales team**, a **time-sensitive trigger**, and **high ticket value**:
- **Storm roofing** — NOAA hail/wind data + door-knocking; 80% of claim-filers pick a contractor within 72h, 78% go with the first responder.
- **Solar** — D2D canvassing orgs (though even here cold cost-per-lead ~$214 is losing to digital ~$125).
- **B2B / adjacency** — suppliers, subs targeting the GC of record, lenders, financing, and **new-mover** home-services marketing.
- **Commercial / high-ticket** — a single $50k+ win justifies a 0.x% hit rate.

The typical small residential remodeler / landscaper / fence crew fits **none** of these.

---

## New-mover & property-data channel (adjacent finding)
New-mover marketing (new homeowners renovate heavily in year one) is a **more established channel** than permits for reaching homeowners *before* they've hired:
- **Data lists (self-serve, cheap):** LeadsPlease (~$0.13–$0.40/record, public pricing), ExactData, Datazapp, Salesgenie, KYC Data.
- **Turnkey mail programs:** Our Town America, Welcomemat (opaque pricing, skew to restaurants/auto, no confirmed Portland operator).
- **Property data:** ATTOM (the one that explicitly markets to home-improvement contractors), PropStream ($99/mo), BatchData, Melissa, First American.
- Direct-mail new-mover response ~4.4% avg — better than cold, still requires mailing budget + follow-through.

---

## The deeper pattern (proven twice now)
Across **both** landscaping and home-improvement, the same wall:
> **What's easy to source (public prospecting data / permits) is low-value. What's high-value (inbound "hire-me" intent) is hard to source (gated behind Nextdoor/FB, or you must generate it yourself).**
This is *why* the hated incumbents (Angi/Thumbtack/LSA) are profitable — they own the scarce, high-value thing: real inbound intent.

---

## Willingness-to-pay benchmarks (for reference)
- Angi/HomeAdvisor: $15–$120/shared lead + ~$300/yr + ~$400/mo min; effective cost-per-*booked-job* often $400–$1,400.
- Thumbtack: ~$8–$200/contact by trade.
- Google LSA: blended ~$53/lead, 43.9% book rate — the cheapest high-intent channel.
- Contractor software: Jobber $49–$349/mo, Housecall Pro $20→$189/mo, ServiceTitan ~$245–398/tech/mo.
- Marketing spend: small local contractor $300–$2,000/mo; rule of thumb 5–15% of revenue.

---

## Honest options forward
1. **Change the customer, not the vertical:** sell permit/prospecting data to the buyers who actually convert on it — B2B suppliers, storm-roofing/solar sales orgs, new-mover home-services. Different (more B2B) business than "help small local contractors."
2. **Chase inbound intent, not cheap data:** the valuable thing is "someone actively wants to hire" — but that's the gated/hard problem (or become the demand-generation front door yourself, the Angi model).
3. **Reconsider the problem space:** lead-gen aggregation for small trades is structurally tough and crowded. A non-lead-gen product for these businesses (or a different customer entirely) may be a better use of the effort.

*Source-quality caveat: pro-permit and pro-inbound conversion figures are largely vendor-authored (optimistic); the strongest disconfirming evidence (unused subscriptions, "never buy a list," low mailer returns) comes from contractor forums and the vendors' own admissions. Reddit/ContractorTalk were bot-blocked, so some forum quotes are search-snippet-level — directional, not primary.*
